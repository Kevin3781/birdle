# Tundra Swan (Cygnus columbianus)
# family: Anatidae  region: north-america
# wikipedia: Tundra swan

The tundra swan (Cygnus columbianus) is a Holarctic species of swan and the smallest of the Holarctic swans. It comprises two subspecies, which are sometimes treated as separate species: Bewick's swan (C. c. bewickii) of the Palaearctic and the whistling swan (C. c. columbianus) of the Nearctic. 
Adult birds of both subspecies have entirely white plumage, black feet, and a mostly black bill with a salmon-pink streak along the mouthline and varying amounts of yellow at the base. Bewick's swan is the smaller subspecies and resembles the whooper swan, while the whistling swan is distinguished by its larger size and nearly all-black bill. The species is notably vocal, producing high-pitched honking calls, particularly when foraging in flocks. 
Tundra swans breed in the tundra of the Arctic and subarctic, inhabiting shallow pools, lakes, and rivers. They are migratory birds, wintering in grassland and marshland habitats, often near coasts. Bewick's swan winters primarily in northwestern Europe and East Asia, while the whistling swan winters along the Pacific and Atlantic coasts of North America. 
The species is monogamous and nests at elevated sites near open water. Clutches typically consist of three to five eggs, with cygnets growing rapidly to fledge before the onset of winter migration. Tundra swans feed mainly on aquatic vegetation in summer and on leftover grain and crops in winter. 
Although the tundra swan is classified as Least Concern by the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN), Bewick's swan populations in northwestern Europe are in decline. Major threats include habitat destruction, water pollution, and hunting both legal and illegal. Lead poisoning from ingested lead shot is also a significant cause of mortality.
